01 Categories of Bullying

Physical Bullying MEDIUM RISK

At ages 5-7, physical bullying typically manifests as hitting, kicking, pushing, shoving, tripping, spitting, taking or damaging belongings, or forcefully taking things from peers. Young children may not distinguish between aggressive play and intentional harm.

Common but often underreported at this age; may be confused with rough play. Research shows it occurs but is less common than verbal/social forms.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Hitting or punching peers during disputes
  • Pushing or shoving on playground or classroom
  • Kicking or tripping peers
  • Spitting on others
  • Taking toys or belongings by force
  • Destroying a peer's property intentionally
  • Getting in physical fights during recess
Warning Signs
  • Unexplained injuries, bruises, or scratches
  • Torn or damaged clothing from school
  • Reports of being hurt by peers
  • Avoidance of physical activities or recess
  • Flinching when approached by peers
Verbal Bullying MEDIUM RISK

Name-calling, teasing, insults, threats, mocking, and mean language directed at peers. At this developmental stage, young children are still learning to regulate emotions and may use verbal aggression impulsively. The line between playful teasing and hurtful bullying can be blurry.

Most frequently reported form of bullying at K-2 level; occurs regularly in early elementary settings.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Name-calling or calling peers mean names
  • Making fun of appearance, clothes, abilities, or family
  • Teasing that continues after child asks it to stop
  • Threatening language or threats of harm
  • Yelling at or mocking peers
  • Making insults about intellect or skills
  • Spreading lies or false statements about peers
Warning Signs
  • Reluctance to speak or participate in class
  • Negative self-talk about intelligence or abilities
  • Becoming withdrawn or quiet
  • Reporting being called mean names by peers
  • Loss of confidence in academic areas
Social/Relational Bullying MEDIUM RISK

Exclusion, social isolation, spreading rumors, and manipulation of peer relationships. At ages 5-7, this emerges as excluding children from games, groups, or friendship circles; intentionally leaving peers out of activities; or refusing to sit with them. Young children are developing social understanding and friendship skills.

Very common at K-2 level; often psychological rather than physical, making it harder for adults to detect.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Refusing to play with or sit near a peer
  • Excluding from games, groups, or activities on purpose
  • Spreading rumors or lies about peers to damage friendships
  • Refusing to be a friend anymore or ultimatums
  • Ignoring or giving the silent treatment
  • Trying to turn other kids against a peer
  • Deliberately keeping peers out of group activities
Warning Signs
  • Eating lunch alone or standing alone at recess
  • Not being invited to birthday parties or playdates
  • Reporting that friends don't want to play with them
  • Increased sadness or tearfulness about friendships
  • Complaints about being left out
  • Anxiety about peer interactions or recess
Cyberbullying LOW RISK

At ages 5-7, cyberbullying is rare and typically limited to devices accessed at school or supervised settings. As this age group has minimal independent technology use, cyberbullying is not yet a primary concern, though it may emerge through shared tablets or classroom technology.

Very rare at K-2 level due to limited technology access; becomes more prevalent in grades 3+.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Mean messages sent through school email or apps (if used)
  • Exclusion from online games or apps at school
  • Sharing embarrassing photos or videos via school devices (rare)
  • Mean comments on school-related online platforms
Warning Signs
  • Reluctance to use school technology
  • Distress after using classroom devices
  • Reporting mean messages from peers online
Identity-Based Bullying HIGH RISK

Bullying targeting race, ethnicity, disability, language, family structure, or other identity characteristics. At K-2 ages, this reflects early awareness of differences and biases children have absorbed. Children are beginning to notice and comment on differences.

Research shows early manifestations; may increase as children age. Children from marginalized groups show higher risk even in early elementary.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Name-calling related to race, ethnicity, or accent
  • Mocking children with disabilities or different abilities
  • Comments about family structure or parents
  • Exclusion based on perceived differences
  • Racist or discriminatory language, even if not fully understood
  • Bullying based on language spoken at home
  • Negative comments about appearance tied to race/ethnicity
Warning Signs
  • Child reporting name-calling related to identity
  • Internalized shame about identity
  • Refusal to speak home language at school
  • Distress about physical characteristics or family
  • Increased anxiety about coming to school

02 Commonalities & Patterns

Who Gets Targeted

Research on K-2 students shows that children with externalizing problem behaviors, low social skills, fewer friendships, and perceived differences are at higher risk for bullying victimization. Vulnerability is common; approximately one-third of young children (ages 5-6) are involved in bullying in some form.

Risk Factors
  • Displays externalizing problem behaviors (aggression, impulsivity, difficulty with rules)
  • Has few close friendships or is socially isolated
  • Lacks assertiveness or difficulty setting boundaries with peers
  • Withdraws from peers or has internalizing problems (anxiety, shyness)
  • Perceived as different from peers (appearance, abilities, family background, race/ethnicity, disability, language)
  • From lower socioeconomic background (family SES is strong predictor)
  • Has difficulty with emotional regulation or expresses emotions negatively
  • Displays reading difficulties or academic struggles
  • Has a disability or special needs (intellectual, developmental, physical, emotional)
  • Immigrant or new to school
  • Lacks parental involvement or support
Protective Factors
  • Has close friendships and is liked by peers
  • Good emotional regulation and self-control
  • Strong academic skills
  • Confident and assertive without aggression
  • From higher socioeconomic background with family support
  • Hispanic ethnicity (research shows protective effect compared to White/Black peers)
  • Involved in activities, hobbies, or sports that build confidence
  • Good relationships with teachers and trusted adults
  • Receives parental support and involvement
  • Demonstrates empathy and social competence

Who Bullies

Bullies at K-2 level are not a uniform group. Research shows two distinct types: those well-connected socially with high status and those socially isolated with emotional/behavioral problems. Externalizing behaviors are the strongest predictor.

Common Traits
  • Externalizing problem behaviors (aggression, poor impulse control, defiance)
  • Difficulty following rules and low behavioral self-regulation
  • Low empathy or difficulty understanding others' feelings
  • History of harsh parenting or family stress
  • Lower socioeconomic status (SES-related correlate)
  • May be well-connected socially OR socially isolated
  • Boys are more likely to be bullies than girls (64.3% vs. 35.7% in some studies)
  • May have experienced bullying or victimization themselves
  • Low self-esteem despite appearing confident
  • Limited ability to identify with emotions of others
Motivations
  • Seeking attention or status among peers
  • Modeling behavior learned at home (harsh parenting practices)
  • Lack of empathy or emotional understanding
  • Frustration with own life circumstances
  • Desire to dominate or control others
  • Imitating bullying behavior witnessed in adults or media
  • Testing social boundaries and power dynamics
  • Reaction to own experiences of victimization (bully-victims)
  • Lack of consequence or adult response to past bullying

Bystander Behavior

Young children at K-2 level often witness bullying but are confused about their role and responsibilities. Many fear intervening will result in retaliation. The blurred line between play and bullying makes identification difficult for young bystanders. Research shows most do not actively help, though few actively support the bully.

Typical Response: Young children often freeze, watch silently, or move away from the situation. Some report bullying to adults, but many do not due to fear of being labeled a tattletale. Few actively intervene to help the victim.

Intervention Rate: Low; estimates suggest only a small percentage of bystanders actively intervene or report bullying to adults. Teacher underestimation is significant (teachers predicted 10% victimization but actual rate was 29%).

Environment & Context

Where It Happens
  • Classroom (39% of bullying occurs here)
  • Hallways or stairwells (37.5%)
  • Playground or outdoor areas during recess (common hot spot)
  • Cafeteria or lunch areas
  • Bathrooms (less supervised areas)
  • School bus or going to/from school
  • Transition times when supervision is minimal
  • Less structured periods like unstructured recess

Gender Patterns: Boys are more likely to be physical bullies and to experience physical victimization (hitting, kicking). Girls are more likely to engage in relational/social bullying and to experience verbal, social, or reputational victimization. The phenomenon is not exclusive to one gender; both boys and girls bully and are victimized. Boys become bully-victims at higher rates (62.5%) than girls (37.3%).

Developmental Context: Ages 5-7 represent a critical transition from home-centered to peer-centered social worlds. Young children are developing theory of mind, emotional regulation, and friendship skills. They are beginning to notice differences among peers but lack the cognitive sophistication to understand social complexity. The distinction between playful teasing and intentional harm is unclear. Peer relationships become increasingly important for identity and belonging. Behavioral impulse control and empathy are still developing. Children at this age are highly influenced by adult modeling and responses. Early elementary is identified as a critical window for prevention and intervention because bullying patterns established here can persist and intensify.

05 Intervention Strategies

Parent Actions

  • Listen to your child without judgment in a safe, supportive space; avoid shaming or blaming them for being bullied
  • Ask open-ended questions about their day, friendships, lunch time, and recess to build awareness of potential bullying
  • Document the bullying incidents in writing (dates, times, what happened, who was involved, what adult was told) to create a history for school discussions
  • Contact your child's teacher and school principal to report the bullying; request specific documentation and a plan to address it
  • Request a meeting with the school to understand their bullying policy and timeline for response (Texas law: notice within 3 business days)
  • Create an action plan with your child about safe responses (walk away, find an adult, stand tall and say 'stop,' find strength in numbers with other kids)
  • Teach your child the difference between tattling (getting someone in trouble) and telling (protecting yourself or others from harm)
  • Help your child build confidence through activities, hobbies, and sports that match their interests and abilities
  • Monitor your child's emotional and behavioral changes; watch for withdrawal, anxiety, reluctance to go to school, sleep changes, or somatic complaints
  • If bullying is identity-based (racial, disability-related, etc.), emphasize your child's identity is not the problem; the bully's behavior is
  • Encourage positive peer relationships by facilitating playdates and social connections outside school
  • Model respectful treatment of others; demonstrate empathy, active listening, and healthy conflict resolution in your own relationships
  • If your child is the bully, have a calm conversation exploring why (not accusatory); teach empathy through role-play and perspective-taking
  • Communicate with the school about your child if they are bullying; ask about consequences that focus on learning and rehabilitation, not humiliation
  • If cyberbullying begins (grade 3+), save evidence, block the person, report to platform, and contact school/law enforcement if threatening

Conversation Starters

  • “"What was one good thing that happened at school today? Any bad things?"”
  • “"Who do you sit with at lunch? Do you like your lunchroom friends?"”
  • “"What do you do at recess? Who do you play with?"”
  • “"Has anyone been mean to you or called you a name at school?"”
  • “"Do you ever feel scared to go to school? Why?"”
  • “"Who are the adults at school you trust most? Who would you tell if something made you uncomfortable?"”
  • “"Do you see other kids being bullied or left out at school?"”
  • “"What does bullying look like? Can you give me an example?"”
  • “"If someone was being mean to you, what would you do? Who would you tell?"”
  • “"Remember, telling a teacher about bullying is NOT tattling. It's helping keep everyone safe. Tattling is when you tell to get someone in trouble for something small."”
  • “"I love you no matter what. If something bad happens, we can figure it out together. You're not in trouble if you tell me."”
  • “"If someone bullies you, you can say 'Stop' in a firm voice, walk away, or go find an adult. You deserve to be treated with respect."”
  • “"Can you tell me about your feelings today? You seem sad/worried/upset."”
  • “"Bullying is never your fault. It's the bully's choice to be mean. That doesn't mean anything is wrong with you."”
  • “"Let's role-play what you could say if someone left you out of a game or called you a name."”

🚨 Red Flags — Act Immediately

  • Child reports being physically hurt or injured by peers (document and report to school and pediatrician)
  • Child mentions threats of harm or weapons at school
  • Child expresses suicidal thoughts, self-harm impulses, or hopelessness related to bullying
  • Child refuses to go to school and shows signs of severe anxiety, panic, or avoidance
  • Child's bullying appears to be identity-based (racial harassment, disability-based, sexual/gender-based) which may violate federal civil rights laws
  • School staff do not respond within the timeline required by state law (Texas: 3 business days for notice) or show resistance to addressing bullying
  • Multiple incidents of bullying occur despite parent and school communication and previous plans
  • Child shows dramatic changes in behavior, grades, sleep, eating, or emotional state linked to bullying
  • Bullying is happening in coordinated group attacks or organized exclusion
  • Evidence of cyberbullying with threatening or sexually explicit content (report to police if applicable)
  • Child reports that school staff witnessed bullying but did not intervene
  • Bully is a student with a pattern of bullying multiple children despite previous consequences

01 Categories of Bullying

Physical Bullying MEDIUM RISK

At ages 8–10, physical bullying includes hitting, kicking, pushing, shoving, tripping, spitting, or intentionally damaging property. At this age, it is often unplanned reactions to peer conflicts but can become more deliberate with practice.

Physical bullying decreases with age; at this elementary stage, it occurs more frequently than in middle/high school. Males report experiencing physical bullying at higher rates (6% vs. 3.7% females nationally).

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Pushing or shoving on playground/in hallways
  • Taking or breaking another child's belongings
  • Hitting or kicking during play escalation
  • Spitting or other gross physical contact
  • Excluding from games or physical activities
Warning Signs
  • Unexplained bruises, scratches, or torn clothing
  • Reluctance to go to recess or specific locations
  • Fear of particular peers or situations
  • Complaints of physical pain (stomach, headaches)
  • Becoming aggressive or retaliatory toward peers
Verbal/Name-Calling Bullying MEDIUM RISK

Name-calling, insults, teasing, and mocking. At ages 8–10, children understand the power of words and intentionally use them to hurt. This is the most common form of bullying reported by this age group.

Verbal bullying is the most frequently reported type across all school grades. At this age, children are developmentally capable of deliberate verbal cruelty but have limited impulse control and empathy.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Persistent name-calling (fat, stupid, ugly, etc.)
  • Mocking appearance, interests, or family background
  • Making jokes at another child's expense
  • Insulting intelligence or athletic ability
  • Public humiliation or embarrassment tactics
  • Teasing that escalates when child shows upset
Warning Signs
  • Child becomes withdrawn or quiet at school
  • Loss of interest in social activities
  • Expressions of feeling 'stupid' or 'ugly'
  • Reluctance to speak up or participate in class
  • Increased anxiety around peers
Social/Relational Bullying HIGH RISK

Spreading rumors, excluding from peer groups, deliberately leaving out, or turning friends against a target. At ages 8–10, this involves subtle social manipulation. Females report higher rates of rumor-based and exclusion bullying (16.6% rumors vs. 9.7% males; 4.9% exclusion vs. 2.6% males).

Relational aggression increases significantly at this age as social awareness develops. Girls are more likely to engage in and be targets of relational bullying.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Spreading false stories or rumors about a child
  • Deliberately excluding from group activities or friend groups
  • Organizing peer rejection (turning others against target)
  • Whispering or giving meaningful looks to make child feel unwelcome
  • Creating exclusive groups designed to include some and exclude others
  • Threatening friendship withdrawal ('if you do this, we won't be your friend')
Warning Signs
  • Child reports 'no one likes me' or 'everyone hates me'
  • Sudden loss of friendships
  • Avoidance of group activities or lunch
  • Comments about what others are saying behind their back
  • Withdrawal and isolation from peer groups
  • Complaints of stomach aches or school avoidance related to social situations
Cyberbullying HIGH RISK

Bullying through digital means (texts, social media, gaming platforms). At ages 8–10, access varies widely; some have devices/social media, others do not. Includes mean messages, exclusion from online groups, sharing embarrassing content, or spreading rumors online.

Cyberbullying is still emerging at this age but increasing. Only 6% of elementary schools report weekly cyberbullying incidents (vs. 37% middle, 25% high school). However, among tweens (ages 10–12), 20.9% report cyberbullying exposure.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Receiving mean or threatening texts/messages
  • Being excluded from online gaming groups or chats
  • Having embarrassing photos or videos shared online
  • Mean comments posted on social media or gaming platforms
  • Rumors spread through text/social media
  • Being blocked or unfriended in an organized, public way
Warning Signs
  • Secretive behavior with devices; quickly closing apps or screens
  • Visible emotional distress after using phone/computer
  • Withdrawal from devices child previously enjoyed
  • Anxiety about checking messages or social media
  • Reluctance to discuss online friendships
  • Sleep disruption (checking phone at night for messages)
Identity-Based Bullying HIGH RISK

Bullying targeting race, ethnicity, religion, disability, perceived sexual orientation, or other identity characteristics. At this age, children are developing awareness of differences and may use identity as a bullying tool without full understanding of impact.

Most common targets: appearance (32.7%), race (13%), disability (9.7%), ethnic origin (9.4%), gender (9.2%), sexual orientation (8.9%), religion (5.8%). Students with disabilities experience 24.5% bullying prevalence in elementary school.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Racial slurs, ethnic stereotypes, or mocking accents
  • Religious mockery (dietary restrictions, holidays, practices)
  • Mocking disabilities, speech impediments, or physical differences
  • Gender-based teasing (too feminine/masculine, clothes, interests)
  • Exclusion based on race, family background, or body type
  • Comments about how a child 'looks different' or 'talks funny'
Warning Signs
  • Child reports being targeted for 'being different'
  • Expressions of shame about cultural or family identity
  • Avoidance of cultural activities or expression
  • Questions about why they 'aren't like other kids'
  • Anxiety or withdrawal around specific groups
  • Internalized negative self-perception related to identity

02 Commonalities & Patterns

Who Gets Targeted

Children ages 8–10 who are bullied often share characteristics of perceived vulnerability, social isolation, or visible differences. At this age, children with low self-esteem, anxiety, or social difficulties are at heightened risk. Physical characteristics (size, appearance), disabilities, and those with fewer friendships are disproportionately targeted.

Risk Factors
  • Low self-esteem; strong predictor of victimization across grade levels
  • Social isolation or few close friendships
  • Visible physical or learning differences
  • Students with disabilities (24.5% bullying rate in elementary)
  • Neurodivergent children (autism, ADHD); autistic students report highest rates among neurodivergent peers
  • Children perceived as physically smaller or weaker
  • Shy, withdrawn, or sensitive temperament
  • Poor social skills or difficulty with peer relationships
  • History of behavioral or externalizing problems in early grades (K-2)
  • Low academic achievement or school engagement
  • Children experiencing family distress, maltreatment, or domestic violence
  • Higher school poverty or attending larger schools
Protective Factors
  • Close, supportive friendships (at least one best friend)
  • Peer social support; peer acceptance buffers bullying risk
  • High self-esteem and positive self-concept
  • Strong family support and open communication with parents
  • Secure attachment to caregivers
  • School connectedness; feeling safe and valued at school
  • Good impulse control and emotional regulation skills
  • Involvement in school activities or extracurricular programs
  • Positive teacher relationships and feeling liked by adults
  • Cultural pride and secure identity

Who Bullies

At ages 8–10, children who bully may do so for various reasons: seeking power/control, low frustration tolerance, modeling behavior witnessed at home, or peer pressure. Unlike older bullies, younger children may not fully understand the harm they cause. Many children who bully also experience bullying themselves (bully-victims).

Common Traits
  • Externalizing behavior problems in K-2 (strong predictor; 0.56 effect size)
  • Higher levels of aggression or impulsivity
  • Lower school performance (paradoxically)
  • Average to above-average self-esteem (differs from victims)
  • Difficulty managing emotions; low frustration tolerance
  • Lack of empathy or difficulty understanding impact on others
  • Need for control or dominance in relationships
  • Peer acceptance or high social status (sometimes)
  • History of harsh parenting, family conflict, or domestic violence exposure
  • Limited problem-solving or conflict resolution skills
Motivations
  • Seeking power, control, or dominance in peer relationships
  • Retaliation or response to their own bullying victimization
  • Peer pressure; maintaining status within a peer group
  • Low frustration tolerance; using bullying as emotional outlet
  • Modeling behavior witnessed at home or in media
  • Lack of empathy; not understanding victim's emotional response
  • Gaining attention or social reward from peers who laugh/encourage
  • Insecurity masked by aggressive behavior
  • Access to resources/power (size, popularity, social influence)

Bystander Behavior

At ages 8–10, most children witness bullying at school (70.6% report witnessing; 71% say bullying is a problem). However, only about 44% of bullied students report it to an adult—lower reporting rate than older children. Bystanders often feel uncertain about how to intervene safely or fear becoming targets themselves.

Typical Response: Bystanders at this age typically either passively watch (fear of retaliation, uncertainty about role), laugh or encourage the bully (seeking social acceptance), or try to distract the bullied child. Very few intervene directly. Younger children (especially 6th-graders) are more likely to report bullying to adults (62%) than older students.

Intervention Rate: Approximately 44% of students overall report bullying to a trusted adult; younger elementary students show higher reporting (6th-graders 62%, 7th-graders 57%). However, willingness to intervene as upstanders (stopping bullying, supporting victim) is estimated at only 10–20% without specific training.

Environment & Context

Where It Happens
  • Classroom (39% of bullying occurs here); often during transitions, independent work, or when teacher is occupied
  • Hallways and stairwells (37.5%); between classes or at passing times
  • Playground/recess (high rate at this age); with minimal adult supervision
  • Cafeteria/lunch (25.1%); crowded, less supervised space
  • School bus (significant at this age); students without adult monitoring
  • Locker rooms or bathrooms; isolated, unsupervised spaces
  • Online spaces (emerging at this age); texts, social media, gaming platforms where children have access

Gender Patterns: Females report higher overall bullying rates (21.8% vs. 16.7% males). Physical bullying is higher among males (6% vs. 3.7% females). Relational/social bullying and rumor-spreading are significantly higher for females (16.6% rumors vs. 9.7% males; 4.9% exclusion vs. 2.6% males). Males and females differ in bullying style: boys engage more in power-based aggression; girls in affiliation-based (social manipulation). Girls experience higher emotional impacts of bullying.

Developmental Context: Ages 8–10 represent a critical window. Children are developing theory of mind (understanding others' mental states) but still have limited empathy and impulse control. Social hierarchy and peer status become increasingly important; exclusion from peer groups causes real distress. Children understand that words hurt and reputation matters. However, they lack the maturity to fully foresee long-term consequences of their actions. This is a 'ripe' age for bullying prevention because interventions at this stage are highly effective; bullying patterns established now often persist if unaddressed.

05 Intervention Strategies

Parent Actions

  • Listen calmly and without judgment when your child discloses bullying; praise them for talking to you
  • Avoid immediate anger or urging retaliation; remain composed to help your child feel safe
  • Believe your child; validate that bullying is real and not their fault
  • Ask open-ended questions to understand full context: 'Tell me what happened. Who was involved? When? How many times?'
  • Document all bullying incidents in writing (dates, times, locations, people involved, what was said/done); keep records for school meetings
  • Contact the school (principal, teacher, counselor) in writing and request a formal meeting to discuss the bullying
  • Request that the school conduct an investigation and provide written documentation of findings and response plan
  • Teach your child three concrete strategies: (1) Tell a trusted adult; (2) Use the 'buddy system'—never be alone; (3) Don't react emotionally when bullied (bullies thrive on reactions)
  • Practice assertiveness and calm responses with role-play: 'That's not OK' then walk away; practice staying calm even when upset
  • Help your child identify one or more trusted peers to sit with at lunch, be with at recess, and spend time with after school
  • Monitor for signs of depression, anxiety, school avoidance; consult school counselor or pediatrician if concerned
  • Encourage participation in activities where your child feels successful (sports, clubs, arts) to build confidence and friendships
  • Talk regularly about school, friendships, and emotions; keep lines of communication open
  • If cyberbullying: keep evidence (screenshots, saved messages); report to platform; consider involving law enforcement if threats present
  • Work with school to develop a written safety plan specifying what the child will do if bullying occurs again
  • Check state and school bullying policies; understand your school's reporting and response procedures
  • If bullying continues or worsens despite school involvement, consider consulting school district administration or state education agency
  • Seek counseling or therapy for your child if showing signs of depression, anxiety, self-harm, or social withdrawal

Conversation Starters

  • “"How was your day? Who did you play with at recess? Any kids being mean today?"”
  • “"I noticed you don't want to go to school today. Is something going on? Is anyone being mean to you?"”
  • “"Tell me about your friends at school. Do you have someone you sit with at lunch?"”
  • “"If someone was being mean to you, how would you want me to help? What would feel safe?"”
  • “"That's not your fault. Being bullied is not because of anything you did or said. The person bullying is making a bad choice."”
  • “"Let's come up with a plan together. What are three things you could do if this happens again?"”
  • “"I'm going to call the school today. We're going to figure this out together."”
  • “"You are not alone. A lot of kids deal with bullying. We're going to get help."”
  • “"I believe you. I'm proud of you for telling me. That took courage."”
  • “"If you see someone else being bullied, it's okay to tell a teacher. That's being a good friend."”
  • “"What do you think you'd like the school to do to make this stop?"”
  • “"Bullying can happen because someone is having a hard time. That doesn't make it okay, but we can try to understand why."”
  • “"Let's practice what you could say. If someone calls you a name, you could say, 'That's not okay,' and then walk away. Want to practice?"”
  • “"Who is someone at school you trust? A teacher, counselor, or friend? Can you go to them if this happens again?"”
  • “"You deserve to feel safe at school. Let's make that happen."”
  • “"I notice you haven't wanted to see your friends as much. Is something going on? Are kids being unkind?"”

  • Child expresses suicidal thoughts or self-harm ideation related to bullying
  • Physical injuries resulting from bullying; threat of or actual violence
  • Child refuses to go to school; extreme school avoidance despite parental intervention

01 Categories of Bullying

Physical Bullying MEDIUM RISK

At ages 11-13, physical bullying includes hitting, pushing, kicking, tripping, shoving, spitting, or forcefully taking belongings. Peak incidence occurs at grade 6, declining through grades 7-8 as bullies shift toward more subtle forms.

Physical bullying peaks in middle school but is less common than verbal or relational forms. Approximately 20.8% of students report any involvement in physical bullying.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Pushing, shoving, tripping in hallways or crowded areas
  • Stealing or damaging belongings (books, backpacks, phones)
  • Forcibly excluding from activities or spaces
  • Physical intimidation in locker rooms or bathrooms
Warning Signs
  • Unexplained bruises, injuries, or torn clothing
  • Damaged personal items (backpack, phone, glasses)
  • Reluctance to attend school or go to specific locations
  • Flinching when touched unexpectedly
Verbal Bullying MEDIUM RISK

Most common form at this age. Includes name-calling, insults, taunting, threatening, mocking, and intimidation using words. At ages 11-13, verbal bullying often shifts from crude insults to more sophisticated put-downs and sarcasm targeting appearance, ability, or background.

Verbal bullying is the most prevalent form, affecting 53.6% of students in some form. Being called names or insulted ranks as one of the top reported bullying experiences (14% of all students).

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Name-calling related to appearance, weight, or disability
  • Mocking accents, interests, or family background
  • Spreading rumors or lies about sexual activity or family
  • Public humiliation through taunting or insults during class changes or lunch
  • Threatening comments about violence or social exclusion
Warning Signs
  • Reluctance to speak in class or participate in discussions
  • Sudden self-consciousness about appearance or abilities
  • Anxiety before school or specific class periods
  • Repeated defensive comments about self-worth
Relational/Social Bullying HIGH RISK

Manipulation of peer relationships through exclusion, gossip, rumor-spreading, and orchestrated isolation. Becomes increasingly sophisticated at ages 11-13. Often involves strategic exclusion from social groups, parties, or group chats. At this age, particularly affects girls who use relational tactics more frequently than boys.

Relational bullying affects 51.4% of students in some form. Rumors (13%) and exclusion are primary relational bullying behaviors at this age tier.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Exclusion from friend groups, lunch tables, or group texts/chats
  • Spreading rumors about relationships, sexuality, or family
  • Deliberate ignoring or "giving the silent treatment"
  • Orchestrating fake friendships to gather information for gossip
  • Excluding from social events or gaming groups online
  • Creating negative social media accounts or group chats about the target
Warning Signs
  • Sudden loss of friends or changes in social circles
  • Anxiety about lunch or unstructured time
  • Obsessive checking of social media or text messages
  • Withdrawal from previously enjoyed social activities
  • Comments about being left out or having no friends
Cyberbullying HIGH RISK

Bullying via digital devices including texting, social media, gaming platforms, and video apps. At ages 11-13, primary platforms are YouTube, TikTok, Snapchat, Minecraft, Roblox, Discord, Instagram, and group messaging. Often overlaps with relational and verbal bullying. Cyberbullying peaks at grades 7-8.

26.5% of students experienced cyberbullying in the past 30 days (2024 data). 21.6% of bullied students report online/text bullying. 37% of middle schools report cyberbullying at least weekly.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Mean or hurtful comments on social media posts (30.4% of cyberbullying victims)
  • Exclusion from group chats or gaming squads (28.9%)
  • Rumors spread online or via text (28.4%)
  • Sharing embarrassing photos/videos without consent (26.9%)
  • Creating fake accounts to impersonate or deceive
  • Recording videos of bullying to share widely
  • Coordinated online campaigns to humiliate someone
Warning Signs
  • Anxiety when phone buzzes or notifications arrive
  • Withdrawal from social media after previously active use
  • Emotional distress tied to specific apps or platforms
  • Hiding phone screen when parents approach
  • Sleep disruption from cyberbullying occurring at any hour
Identity-Based/Bias Bullying HIGH RISK

Targeting students for race, ethnicity, religion, gender identity, sexual orientation, disability, or other personal characteristics. At ages 11-13, identity-based bullying often increases in frequency and intensity as peer hierarchies solidify and misinformation spreads. LGBTQ+ students, students of color, and students with disabilities are disproportionately targeted.

13% of bullying targets report appearance-based bullying; 9.7% report disability-based bullying; 13% race-based; 9.2% gender-based; 8.9% sexual orientation-based. LGBTQ+ students report 82% feeling unsafe due to bullying.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Racial slurs, mockery of accent or cultural practices
  • Exclusion based on religion or family background
  • Comments about gender expression, sexuality, or coming out
  • Mockery of disability accommodations (crutches, hearing aids, service animals)
  • Harassment related to socioeconomic status (hand-me-downs, free lunch program)
  • Organized campaigns targeting specific identity groups
Warning Signs
  • Avoiding discussion of family background or identity
  • Hypervigilance about appearance related to identity
  • Expressed wish to change appearance, name, or identity
  • Isolating from cultural or identity-based peer groups
  • Comments about not feeling safe at school

02 Commonalities & Patterns

Who Gets Targeted

At ages 11-13, approximately 26.1% of middle school students experience bullying. Targets are often those perceived as socially vulnerable, different, or lower in peer status. Vulnerability increases with multiple overlapping risk factors.

Risk Factors
  • Low peer social status or social isolation
  • Low self-esteem or high social anxiety
  • Students with disabilities (44.4% vs 31.3% without disabilities)
  • LGBTQ+ or gender-nonconforming students (47.1% vs 30% cisgender/heterosexual)
  • Students of color, particularly Black students in predominantly white schools
  • Students with lower socioeconomic status or visible markers of poverty
  • Immigrant or refugee students
  • Students with poor social skills or difficulty reading social cues
  • Overweight or obese students
  • Students with mental health conditions (anxiety, depression)
  • New students or those transitioning schools
  • Students perceived as 'uncool' by peer standards at this age
  • Those with unusual interests, speech patterns, or appearance
Protective Factors
  • Strong peer friendships and social support network
  • Positive relationships with at least one trusted adult
  • High academic performance and school engagement
  • Involvement in structured activities (sports, clubs, music)
  • Strong family support and involvement
  • Ability to use humor effectively (self-deprecation, not sarcasm at others' expense)
  • Assertiveness and social skills to set boundaries
  • Cultural or community identity pride and support
  • Access to mental health support or counseling
  • School environment with strong anti-bullying policies and bystander support
  • Developing coping and stress management strategies

Who Bullies

Not a homogenous group. Some bullies are socially dominant and popular; others are socially marginalized. 13% of students bully in early adolescence. Bullying behavior peaks in middle school (grades 6-8) before declining in high school. Approximately 10% of bullies exhibit consistently high rates over time.

Common Traits
  • Seek dominance and social status through aggression
  • Often have well-developed social skills and peer influence
  • May lack empathy or show positive attitudes toward violence
  • Use bullying to gain or maintain peer group position
  • Some are victims of bullying themselves (bully-victims, 6.3% of population)
  • Higher rates among males for physical/verbal bullying
  • Higher rates among females for relational bullying
  • Some bullies are socially isolated and have few friends
  • May come from homes with harsh discipline or low parental involvement
  • Some experience their own trauma or adverse home conditions
  • Often struggle with emotional regulation
  • May bully to deflect from their own vulnerabilities
Motivations
  • Desire to achieve or maintain high social status and peer dominance
  • Retaliation or revenge for perceived slights
  • Modeling behavior witnessed at home or in community
  • Gaining attention and notoriety among peers
  • Coping mechanism for their own anxiety or low self-esteem
  • Desire for peer acceptance and belonging
  • Entertainment or boredom relief
  • Defensive protection against being bullied themselves
  • Displacement of anger or stress from other situations
  • Experimenting with power and aggression during developmental stage

Bystander Behavior

100% of students report experiencing, witnessing, or being aware of bullying during the school year. Most students (60-70%) witness bullying but do not intervene. Bystanders can play multiple roles: passive bystanders, reinforcers of bullying, assistants to bullies, or defenders. At ages 11-13, peer pressure strongly influences bystander response.

Typical Response: Most common bystander responses at this age include: doing nothing or walking away (most frequent), watching without intervening, laughing along with bullies to gain social acceptance, or subtly encouraging bullying through silent approval. Fewer than 20% actively defend the target. Fear of becoming a target themselves is the primary barrier to intervention.

Intervention Rate: Approximately 10-20% of bystanders actively intervene or defend victims. Rates are higher among students with strong friendships, those in schools with positive anti-bullying climates, and those taught specific intervention strategies. Girls are slightly more likely to intervene than boys.

Environment & Context

Where It Happens
  • Classroom (47% of bullying occurs here)
  • Hallways and stairwells (39%)
  • Cafeteria and lunch areas (26-30%)
  • Outside on school grounds (20%)
  • Locker rooms and bathrooms (less frequent but significant for sexual harassment/assault)
  • On school buses and bus stops
  • On the way to/from school
  • Unstructured/unsupervised time (transitions between classes, recess)
  • Digital spaces (social media, group chats, gaming platforms, text messages) - increasingly common, can reach victims 24/7

Gender Patterns: At ages 11-13, girls report higher overall bullying rates (22% vs 17% for boys). Girls are bullied more frequently for appearance (high priority at this age due to puberty), exclusion, and rumor-spreading. Boys experience more physical bullying. Girls more likely to be cyberbullied and report online/text bullying (27.7% vs 14.1% for boys). Both genders participate in all bullying forms, but females more frequently use relational aggression; males more frequently use physical aggression.

Developmental Context: Ages 11-13 mark critical developmental transitions: puberty onset, brain reorganization prioritizing peer relationships over family, increased abstract reasoning enabling more sophisticated social manipulation, developing identity formation, emerging sexual awareness and gender consciousness. Peer acceptance becomes paramount; social hierarchies rigidify. Empathy is developing but still inconsistently applied. Technology integration accelerates social connection and vulnerability. This is the peak age for bullying onset and victimization risk.

05 Intervention Strategies

Parent Actions

  • Listen without judgment when your child discloses bullying; avoid immediate problem-solving or blame responses
  • Validate your child's emotions and reassure them the bullying is not their fault
  • Ask open-ended questions to understand the full scope: who, when, where, how often, what was said/done, who witnessed it
  • Document all incidents in writing including dates, times, locations, descriptions, witnesses, and child's emotional/physical reactions
  • Request a meeting with school administration (principal, counselor, or anti-bullying coordinator) within 24-48 hours; bring documentation
  • Get written explanation of school's investigation findings and response plan; request timeline for addressing the issue
  • Work with school to develop a monitoring plan with regular check-ins (weekly initially, then bi-weekly)
  • Help child develop assertiveness and boundary-setting skills through role-play of specific scenarios
  • Practice stress-management techniques together: deep breathing, visualization, journaling, physical exercise
  • Maintain open communication about school and social life; ask specifically about bullying, not just 'how was school'
  • Connect child with trusted adult at school (counselor, coach, teacher) as additional support person
  • Arrange peer support: encourage involvement in clubs, sports, or activities where child has positive peer connections
  • Consider individual counseling or therapy if child shows signs of depression, anxiety, or significant distress
  • Monitor academic performance and notify teacher if grades decline or engagement drops
  • Limit social media use if cyberbullying is occurring; set boundaries on device access, especially before sleep
  • Save evidence of cyberbullying (screenshots) and report to platform; consider reporting to police if threats are made
  • Avoid confronting the bully or their parents directly; channel communication through school
  • File formal complaint with Office of Civil Rights if school does not respond adequately and civil rights violations occurred
  • Request IEP or 504 Plan meeting if child has disability; include bullying safety plan and accommodations
  • Model healthy conflict resolution and respectful communication in your own interactions

Conversation Starters

  • “I've noticed you seem different since school started/lately. Is something happening at school that's worrying you?”
  • “I saw a situation on TV about bullying. Has anything like that ever happened to you or someone you know?”
  • “How do you feel about your friend group right now? Are there any friends you're worried about or having trouble with?”
  • “Some kids at your age told me they feel nervous about certain places at school—the bathroom, locker room, bus. Do any places make you anxious?”
  • “I want you to know that if anything ever happens that makes you feel uncomfortable or unsafe, I want to hear about it. You won't get in trouble for telling me.”
  • “It's not your fault if someone bullies you. Bullying says something about the other person, not about you.”
  • “How do you handle it when someone is mean to you or says something hurtful? What do you usually do?”
  • “Have you ever felt left out by friends or peers? What was that like, and did you talk to anyone about it?”
  • “I know social media can be a place where people say hurtful things. Have you or anyone you know experienced that?”
  • “If you saw someone being bullied, what would you do? How would that feel?”
  • “You're growing up and learning about yourself. What parts of yourself do you worry about others judging?”
  • “Sometimes kids bully because they're struggling with their own stuff. That doesn't make it okay, but it helps to understand.”
  • “I'm always on your side. Even if something embarrassing happened or you said something you regret, I want to help you figure it out.”
  • “Tell me about the best and hardest parts of your day today.”
  • “Is there anyone at school who makes you feel good about yourself? Who are your allies?”

🚨 Red Flags — Act Immediately

  • Threats of violence or self-harm explicitly stated by child or in messages
  • Weapons brought to school or mention of weapons in context of bullying
  • Severe physical injury from bullying incident (requiring emergency room visit)
  • Child expressing suicidal ideation or making suicide gestures/attempts
  • Sudden severe behavioral change: aggressive outbursts, extreme withdrawal, inability to function at home/school
  • Unexplained injuries, bruises, or signs of physical abuse appearing regularly
  • Sexual harassment, sexual assault, or explicit sexual images being shared
  • School refusing to investigate or respond after formal complaint filed
  • Cyberbullying escalating despite attempts to stop it; evidence of organized coordinated harassment
  • Child being excluded so completely that social isolation is total (no friends, no lunch companion, completely alone)
  • Evidence of eating disorder, severe sleep disruption, or self-harm behaviors emerging after bullying begins
  • Bullying based on protected characteristics (race, disability, gender identity, sexual orientation) with potential civil rights violation
  • School expressing indifference to bullying or victim-blaming responses
  • Child refusing to attend school or expressing extreme terror about specific location/person
  • Retaliatory bullying increasing after reporting to school; retaliation against child for reporting
  • Multiple bullies coordinating against single target; mob-like behavior
  • Bullying extending beyond school into community, home, or 24/7 via internet with no safe spaces

01 Categories of Bullying

Verbal/Social Bullying (Relational) HIGH RISK

At ages 14-18, verbal and relational bullying manifest as spreading rumors, social exclusion, name-calling, and deliberate social ostracism. High schoolers use sophisticated social strategies including exclusion from group chats, dismissive comments on social media, and deliberate isolation. This form exploits peer status and social networks.

Most common form at all grade levels; spreading rumors (13%) and being made fun of or called names (11.9%) are leading behaviors. Social exclusion is particularly damaging at this age.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Spreading rumors or false stories about peer relationships, appearance, or sexual behavior
  • Exclusion from social groups, parties, and online group chats
  • Publicly humiliating peers through name-calling, insults, or mockery
  • Circulating embarrassing photos or videos with intent to harm reputation
  • Making disparaging comments about physical appearance, sexuality, or identity
  • Deliberately isolating a peer from friend groups using social pressure
Warning Signs
  • Sudden withdrawal from social activities or friend groups
  • Changes in mood when discussing school or peers
  • Reluctance to attend social events or school
  • Comments about feeling excluded or unpopular
  • Anxiety around peer interactions
  • Decline in self-esteem and negative self-talk
Cyberbullying HIGH RISK

For high schoolers (14-18), cyberbullying occurs primarily through social media platforms (TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat), gaming environments, and text messaging. This form is 24/7, often anonymous, and leaves permanent digital records. Tactics include hurtful comments on posts, exclusion from group chats, creation of memes targeting individuals, and threats.

26.5% of students experienced cyberbullying in previous 30 days (2024). Lifetime prevalence has doubled from 18.8% (2007) to 54.6% (2023) among ages 13-17. Higher rates for females (21%) vs. males (12%) in high school.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Mean or hurtful comments posted on social media (30.4% of those bullied)
  • Exclusion from group chats or gaming servers (28.9%)
  • Rumors spread online (28.4%)
  • Embarrassing or humiliating content shared (26.9%)
  • False rumors about relationships or sexuality spread via messaging
  • Non-consensual sharing of intimate or embarrassing photos
  • Online threats of violence or self-harm
  • Creating fake accounts to impersonate or mock
Warning Signs
  • Excessive phone use followed by emotional distress
  • Sudden withdrawal from social media or gaming
  • Checking phone anxiously or hiding screen
  • Sleep disturbances or anxiety about online interactions
  • Reluctance to discuss online activities
  • Emotional reactions to notifications or messages
Physical Bullying HIGH RISK

Physical bullying at high school ages is less frequent than in middle school but includes hitting, pushing, threats of violence, and property damage. Males experience physical bullying at higher rates (6% vs. females 3.7%). May be used to establish dominance or control.

Lower than verbal/social forms; males 6% vs. females 3.7% in 2021-22. Decreases from middle school (25%) to high school (15.7%) overall prevalence.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Physical pushing, shoving, or hitting
  • Threats of physical harm or violence
  • Intimidation using size or physical strength
  • Destruction of personal property
  • Violent gestures or threatening body language
  • Cornering or physically trapping a peer
Warning Signs
  • Unexplained bruises, cuts, or injuries
  • Torn or damaged clothing or belongings
  • Fear of specific individuals or locations
  • Avoidance of certain areas of school
  • Flinching at sudden movements
  • Defensive posture when near certain peers
Identity-Based Bullying HIGH RISK

Bullying targeting sexual orientation, gender identity, race, ethnicity, disability, religion, or socioeconomic status. At high school age, this is particularly pronounced for LGBTQ+ students (29% vs. 16% for heterosexual/cisgender students). Includes bias-motivated harassment, racial slurs, and discrimination.

LGBTQ+ high schoolers 29% bullied at school vs. 16% cisgender/heterosexual peers. 25% LGBTQ+ students electronically bullied vs. 13% others. Black students face race-based targeting; 21% of Black teens report being targeted online because of race/ethnicity.

Age-Specific Behaviors
  • Slurs or derogatory language based on protected characteristics
  • Exclusion from activities based on identity
  • Mocking appearance, clothing, or cultural practices
  • Assumptions about sexuality or gender identity
  • Discriminatory comments about race, ethnicity, or national origin
  • Targeting based on religious beliefs or practices
  • Bullying related to socioeconomic status or family circumstances
Warning Signs
  • Increased anxiety around social identity or peer groups
  • Avoiding discussion of identity or background
  • Isolation from communities that share their identity
  • Expressions of shame or self-directed negative statements about identity
  • Reluctance to participate in cultural or religious activities
  • Emotional distress when identity is discussed or targeted

02 Commonalities & Patterns

Who Gets Targeted

High school bullying targets are often perceived as different, vulnerable, or lacking social power. Certain demographic groups face disproportionate risk. Targets may be quiet, introverted, socially isolated, or exhibit characteristics that mark them as outside dominant peer groups.

Risk Factors
  • LGBTQ+ identity (29% vs. 16% heterosexual/cisgender students)
  • Students of color, particularly Black and multiracial students (30% vs. 9% Asian students)
  • Low socioeconomic status and poverty
  • Students with disabilities or different abilities
  • Immigrant or refugee status
  • Perceived social isolation or small friend groups
  • Physical appearance or weight differences
  • Mental health conditions or neurodivergence
  • Introverted, shy, or anxious temperament
  • Low self-esteem or negative self-perception
  • Perceived weakness or inability to defend oneself
Protective Factors
  • Strong peer support and positive friendships
  • Parental involvement and open family communication
  • School belonging and positive teacher relationships
  • Involvement in extracurricular activities and hobbies
  • Confidence and assertiveness skills
  • Connection to cultural, religious, or community groups
  • High academic engagement
  • Sense of school safety and inclusion
  • Access to school counselors or support services
  • Resilience and coping skills

Who Bullies

High school bullies are not necessarily socially isolated or unpopular; many report having friends and strong social connections. They often seek power, status, and dominance within peer hierarchies. By age 14, bullying becomes more about maintaining social position than reactive aggression. Males are more likely to be classified as bullies, though females engage in relational aggression at similar or higher rates.

Common Traits
  • Average to above-average self-esteem
  • Social connection and peer support (more friends than targets)
  • Desire for power, dominance, or social status
  • Proactive aggression (planned, goal-directed)
  • Confidence and assertiveness, sometimes excessive
  • Traits of sadism or low empathy in some cases
  • Experience with aggression in home environment
  • Involvement in other risk behaviors or substance use
  • Lower academic performance in some cases
  • Difficulty accepting responsibility or accountability
Motivations
  • Establishing or maintaining social dominance and status
  • Gaining peer approval and acceptance
  • Deflecting attention from own problems or insecurities
  • Retaliation for perceived slights or conflicts
  • Demonstrating power in response to powerlessness elsewhere
  • Reinforcing group cohesion through shared mockery
  • Entertainment or excitement-seeking
  • Expressing prejudice or bias beliefs
  • Compensating for low status by targeting vulnerable peers

Bystander Behavior

At high school age, bystanders are present in most bullying situations. Research indicates that peer intervention can stop bullying in more than half of incidents. However, only about 44% of students who are bullied notify an adult at school, with older grades (11-12) reporting even lower rates (32-41% vs. 62-57% in 6-7). Many bystanders experience anxiety, guilt, or helplessness. Social dynamics and fear of retaliation influence intervention rates.

Typical Response: Passive observation is most common; silent presence without intervention. Some bystanders may join perpetrators to avoid becoming targets themselves (social dominance theory). Older teens (16-18) are less likely to report to adults than younger high schoolers.

Intervention Rate: More than 50% of bullying stops when a peer intervenes, but only a minority of bystanders actively intervene. High school students may fear retaliation, social exclusion, or escalation if they intervene. Reporting to adults is even rarer among high schoolers.

Environment & Context

Where It Happens
  • Classroom and during instruction (39% of bullying occurs inside classroom)
  • Hallways and stairwells (37.5% of bullying)
  • Cafeteria and lunch areas
  • School grounds and outdoor areas
  • School buses and transportation
  • Locker rooms and bathrooms
  • Parking lots and areas near campus
  • Online (social media, group chats, gaming platforms) - 24/7
  • Events and assemblies
  • Off-campus locations where peers congregate
  • Virtual learning spaces if school is hybrid or remote

Gender Patterns: At high school ages, females report higher overall bullying rates (21.8% vs. 16.7% males) and much higher rates of cyberbullying (21% vs. 12% males among those bullied). Females are more targeted for rumors (16.6% vs. 9.7% males) and social exclusion (4.9% vs. 2.6% males). Males experience more physical bullying (6% vs. 3.7% females). Girls ages 15-17 show especially high cyberbullying rates (54% vs. 44% boys same age). Gender-based bullying and sexual harassment are also concerns at this developmental stage.

Developmental Context: High school ages (14-18) coincide with peak identity formation, increasing peer influence, and transition to adult-like social hierarchies. Adolescents are developmentally sensitive to peer rejection and social exclusion. At age 14, proactive aggression becomes strongly tied to bullying others (more than reactive responses in earlier years). Social status and peer hierarchy become organizing principles of school social life. Identity exploration (sexual orientation, gender, racial/ethnic identity) makes some teens vulnerable to bias-based bullying. Increasing autonomy and time spent with peers (vs. family) elevates bullying risk. Online life becomes central to social identity and peer relationships, creating new vulnerabilities for cyberbullying. Brain development in areas related to empathy and impulse control is still ongoing, while reward-seeking and sensation-seeking peak.

05 Intervention Strategies

Parent Actions

  • Listen calmly and without judgment when your teen discloses bullying; validate their feelings before taking action
  • Ask specific questions about what happened, who was involved, where/when it occurred, and how long it's been happening; write down details and dates
  • Assure your teen it is not their fault and that you will help protect them and address the situation
  • Avoid advising them to fight back or retaliate; explain that this escalates situations and puts them at legal/physical risk
  • Contact the school in writing (email is best) to report the bullying and request an investigation; include specific incidents and dates
  • Request a meeting with teachers, counselors, and administrators to create a written bullying intervention plan
  • If cyberbullying, document everything (screenshots, saved messages, photos) and preserve evidence before the platform deletes content
  • Work with school to implement monitoring and safety plans; request daily check-ins from school staff
  • Help your teen develop concrete strategies: buddy system, avoiding locations/times of bullying, using specific responses ('stop, that's not ok,' then walk away)
  • Role-play challenging social situations and safe intervention strategies; practice confident body language and calm tone
  • Encourage involvement in extracurricular activities outside school to build confidence and alternative peer relationships
  • Monitor online activities and social media; discuss digital citizenship and how to block/report users
  • Watch for signs of depression, anxiety, or self-harm; consider counseling or therapy if bullying impacts mental health
  • If school does not respond adequately within 1-2 weeks, escalate to principal, superintendent, or district office
  • Know your state bullying laws (check StopBullying.gov) and school district policies; reference them when communicating with school
  • In Texas, reference David's Law (TEC §37.0832) and know that schools must investigate and document bullying
  • Maintain ongoing communication with your teen about school; ask open-ended questions regularly, not just about bullying
  • If bullying is identity-based (LGBTQ+, race, disability), connect with advocacy groups and ensure school compliance with civil rights laws
  • Document all communications with school (keep emails, record meeting dates/attendees, save copies of all incident reports)
  • If bullying is severe, ongoing, or school response inadequate, consider changing schools or filing formal complaint with Office of Civil Rights (federal level)
  • Support your teen's mental health: normalize counseling, monitor for depression/anxiety symptoms, ensure adequate sleep and exercise
  • Model respectful conflict resolution and kindness in your own relationships; children learn by observing parents
  • Create a family environment free from harsh discipline, yelling, or conflict; home modeling affects bullying behavior significantly

Conversation Starters

  • “I've noticed you seem sad/anxious about school lately. What's been going on? I'm here to listen without judgment.”
  • “Do you ever feel left out at school or worried about how peers treat you? You can tell me anything.”
  • “Have you seen anyone being treated badly by their friends? How did that make you feel?”
  • “If someone was being mean to you online or at school, what would feel like the best way to handle it?”
  • “What does bullying look like at your school? Who would you talk to if something happened to you?”
  • “I want you to know that if you're ever bullied, it's never your fault. How can I help if that happens?”
  • “Let's talk about what makes a good friend. What qualities do you value in friendships?”
  • “How are things with your friend group? Do you feel safe and happy when you're together?”
  • “If you saw someone being bullied online, what would you do? How could you help without putting yourself at risk?”
  • “Tell me about someone at school who is treated differently or doesn't fit in. How do you think they feel?”
  • “Have you ever felt pressured to be mean to someone or go along with rumors? What happened?”
  • “What's your school doing about bullying and cyberbullying? Do you feel like it's effective?”
  • “Teens say cyberbullying is a bigger problem now. Have you or your friends ever experienced mean comments, rumors, or exclusion online?”
  • “I want to understand your social media and gaming world better. What platforms do you use most? Who are you connected with?”
  • “If something really upset or scared you online, would you come talk to me? I won't take your phone or get mad.”
  • “Let's talk about how to respond if someone sends you a mean message or posts something hurtful about you.”
  • “Do you know how to block, report, or save evidence of cyberbullying? Let's go through it together.”
  • “What would help you feel more confident at school? What are you good at that we could build on?”

🚨 Red Flags — Act Immediately

  • Any mention of suicidal thoughts, self-harm, or hopelessness (contact 988 or Emergency Services immediately)
  • Threats of violence toward self or others (contact school, law enforcement, and mental health crisis team)
  • Physical injuries, marks, or bruises that student cannot explain or attributes to bullying
  • Sudden, dramatic change in mood, behavior, or personality (withdrawal, aggression, isolation)
  • Refusal to attend school or extreme anxiety about school attendance
  • Severe sleep disturbances, appetite changes, or neglect of personal hygiene
  • Possession or discussion of weapons or violent ideation
  • Significant academic decline or loss of interest in previously enjoyed activities
  • Substance use or acting out behaviors that intensify after bullying disclosure
  • Non-consensual sharing of intimate images or explicit content (contact police and school immediately)
  • Cyberbullying involving threats, extortion, or coercion (sextortion, blackmail)
  • Evidence of coordinated group bullying or organized harassment (hate group, conspiracy)
  • Bullying involving identity-based threats or slurs that could constitute hate crimes
  • School's failure to respond to bullying report within reasonable timeframe (1-2 weeks)
  • Pattern of escalating bullying despite school intervention
  • Student expressing feeling hopeless about situation or believing bullying will never stop
  • Bullying occurring off-campus with school awareness and failure to address (cyberbullying, transportation, events)
  • Any incident involving physical violence, weapons, or threats of serious harm
  • Evidence of bullying by school staff or authority figures (abuse of power)
  • Student becoming withdrawn and isolated from all peer relationships and support systems
